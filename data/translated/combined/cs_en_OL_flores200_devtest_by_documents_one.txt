When a small group of animals (a small population) separates from the main population it originates from (for example, when they cross a mountain ridge or river or move to a new island from which it is not easy to get back), they often find themselves in a different environment than they were before. This new environment has different resources and different competitors, so the new population will need different characteristics or ways of adapting to become a stronger competitor than in the past. The indigenous population has not changed at all, they still need the same adaptations as before. Over time, as a new population adapts to its new environment, it begins to look less and less like other populations. Eventually, in a few thousand or even millions of years, the two populations will be so different that they can no longer be considered the same species. We call this process speciation, which means the emergence of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and a very important part of evolution.
