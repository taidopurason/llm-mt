When a small group of living organisms (a small population) moves away from the main population from which it originates (for example, if it moves over a mountain range, a river, or moves to a new island and it is difficult to return), it will often find itself in a different environment than it was previously.
This new environment has different sources and competitors, so the new population will need different characteristics or adaptations to be able to compete with them.
The original population has not changed in any way, and still requires the same adaptations as before.
When the new population begins to adapt to the new environment, it becomes increasingly different from the other population.
Over time, it becomes less and less similar to the other population, and after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations will have become so different that they can no longer be considered the same species.
This process is called speciaction, which means the formation of new species. Speciaction is an inevitable consequence and an important part of evolution.
