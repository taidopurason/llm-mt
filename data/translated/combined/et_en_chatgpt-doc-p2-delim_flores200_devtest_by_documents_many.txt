When a small group of organisms (a small population) becomes isolated from the main population they originated from (for example, by crossing a mountain range or a river, or by moving to a new island where returning is not easy), they often find themselves in a different environment.
In the new environment, there are different resources and different competitors, so the new population needs different traits than the ones that were previously necessary, and it must adapt in order to be a strong competitor.
The original population has not changed at all, they still need the same adaptations as before.
Over time, as the new population adapts to its new environment, they start to look less and less like the other population.
Eventually, after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations look so different that they cannot be called the same species.
We call this process speciation, which simply means the emergence of a new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence of evolution and a very important part of it.
