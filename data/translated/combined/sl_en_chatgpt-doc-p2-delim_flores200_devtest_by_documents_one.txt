When a small group of living organisms (a small population) becomes separated from the main population it originated from (for example, by crossing a mountain range or a river, or by moving to a new island where it is difficult to return), it will often find itself in a different environment than it previously lived in. This new environment has different resources and competitors, so the new population will need different traits or adaptations in order to compete with them. The original population has not changed in any way, they still need the same adaptations as before. As the new population begins to adapt to the new environment, it gradually becomes less similar to the other population. Over time, after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations will appear so different that they cannot be considered the same species. This process is called speciation, which means the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and a very important part of evolution.
