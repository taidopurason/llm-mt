After the Congress had adopted it on July 4, the manuscript of the Congress President John Hancock and Secretary Charles Thomson was sent to the printing house of John Dunlap, a few quarters away.
During the night, 150 to 200 copies were made, now known as copies of Dunlap.
The first public reading of the document was conducted by John Nixon on July 8 in the Independence Hall.
One was sent to George Washington on July 6, who let his soldiers read on July 9 in New York.Its copy reached London on August 10.
25 Copies made by Dunlap are the oldest preserved copies of the declaration.The original handwritten copy has not been preserved.
